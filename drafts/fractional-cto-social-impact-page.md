## Engineering Decisions Are Mission Decisions

I grew up as a first-generation immigrant. I've seen firsthand what access to good education and technology can do — and what its absence costs. That's why, after years at Google and in edtech, I think about technical choices differently than most CTOs.

When a startup building workforce development software chooses a data architecture, they're not just making a performance tradeoff. They're deciding how much user data they'll collect, who can see it, and what happens to learners if the company pivots or folds. When a health equity platform picks a third-party vendor, they're deciding whose infrastructure their most vulnerable users will depend on. When a fintech startup targeting underserved communities cuts corners on accessibility, they're making a decision about who gets to use the product.

These aren't edge cases. They're the everyday engineering work of mission-driven companies — and they require someone who has thought them through before, not someone learning on the job.

*— Yu Chen, Co-Founder, Potluck Technologies*

## What "Mission-Driven Fractional CTO" Actually Means

The phrase gets used loosely. Here's what it means in practice, and what it doesn't.

It doesn't mean a CTO who charges less because your work is important. It doesn't mean someone who lacks technical depth but cares a lot. And it doesn't mean a fractional CTO who lists "nonprofits" as an industry they'll serve alongside everyone else.

It means a fractional CTO who understands that in your organization, the wrong technical call has consequences that go beyond churn rate or revenue. It means someone who has built at the intersection of technology and access before — not someone adapting general consulting experience to your context.

At Potluck, our social impact work is grounded in direct experience:

- **Google Translate** (Sha-Mayn): a product that gave hundreds of millions of people access to information in their own language. Every architectural decision about language model quality, latency, and coverage had implications for who could actually use it.
- **Google connectivity programs** (Yu): expanding internet access in underserved markets across EMEA and APAC, where infrastructure constraints, cost, and trust were all product problems.
- **TeachersPayTeachers** (Sha-Mayn): an edtech marketplace serving K-12 teachers, many in underfunded schools. Building a 50-person engineering org there meant keeping the product reliable and affordable for users who didn't have much margin for error.
- **Flatiron School** (Yu): workforce development for career changers, many from non-traditional backgrounds. Product decisions about curriculum, pacing, and outcomes data directly affected whether people got jobs.

This isn't impact-washing. It's the actual work we did before starting Potluck.

## Who Potluck Works With

We've found that three types of organizations are good fits for what we do. They're related but distinct.

### Mission-Driven For-Profit Startups

These are companies built with a clear social thesis alongside (or embedded in) their business model. Think: workforce development platforms, financial inclusion fintechs, health equity tools, civic tech products, accessibility-first software. Revenue-generating, raising from venture or impact investors, building for scale.

This is our primary focus. You need a CTO who can hold the technical strategy and the mission framing simultaneously — because your investors and your partners are going to ask about both.

### B Corps and Social Enterprises

Certified B Corps and similar entities often reach a technical inflection point — usually when the product needs to scale, when the engineering team has outgrown its early setup, or when a major enterprise partner starts asking questions about your infrastructure. We've helped companies at exactly these junctures.

The specific considerations here: your B Corp certification has supply chain and governance implications that affect vendor and infrastructure choices. We know this.

### Nonprofit Tech Teams

We work selectively with nonprofits that have built internal technology products or platforms — not nonprofits that primarily use off-the-shelf tools. If your nonprofit has an engineering team building proprietary software, you may need fractional CTO leadership to manage that team and make the right architectural calls. If you're primarily evaluating SaaS vendors, that's a different kind of help and not what we do best.

## Who We're Not the Right Fit For

We'd rather tell you this upfront than waste your time.

**Organizations that need a nonprofit technology consultant.** If you need help selecting a CRM, migrating to a new donor management system, or evaluating SaaS tools for program delivery — that's legitimate and important work, but it's not what we do. There are excellent firms focused on this.

**UK-based charities and social enterprises.** We're US-focused. We don't have deep knowledge of the UK regulatory environment, the charity commission requirements, or the UK funding landscape. There are good fractional CTO options in the UK; we're not one of them.

**Organizations that need a full-time CTO immediately.** If you've scaled to the point where you need a full-time technical leader in the building every day, hire one. We'll tell you when we think that's the case, and we'll help you think through what to look for.

**Startups where the mission is a marketing layer.** We're not the CTO for companies that put "social impact" in their pitch deck but don't build it into their product decisions. We'll figure this out quickly in discovery, and it won't be a good match.

## What We Do

### Fractional CTO Engagement

We embed inside your team. This isn't advisory from a distance — it means attending standups, reviewing PRs, sitting in on investor meetings, and being available when something breaks at 11pm.

What we focus on for mission-driven companies:

**Technical strategy aligned to your mission constraints.** Your data ethics requirements, your accessibility standards, your cost structure, your trust obligations to users — these all have technical implications. We map them explicitly and make sure your architecture reflects them.

**Build vs. buy decisions with downstream thinking.** Every vendor you adopt is a dependency. For mission-driven companies, vendor dependencies carry additional risk: pricing changes that affect accessibility, data practices that conflict with your values, companies that get acquired. We've seen this play out. We help you think through it before you're locked in.

**Engineering team leadership and hiring.** Sha-Mayn built and led a 50-person engineering org. We know how to hire engineers who can execute, and how to build team cultures that don't burn people out chasing a mission.

**Investor and partner diligence.** Impact investors ask different questions than traditional VCs. Enterprise partners in regulated sectors (healthcare, education, government) ask detailed questions about security, compliance, and infrastructure. We prepare you for these conversations and participate in them.

### Advisory

Weekly or biweekly sessions for organizations that already have technical leadership but want a senior thought partner. We work well in this mode with CTOs who are early in their careers, founding engineers who've grown into technical leadership roles, and non-technical founders who want a second opinion on major technical decisions.

### Coaching

Yu and Sha-Mayn both coach technical leaders and founders. Sha-Mayn is an ICF-certified coach. If you're a CTO or technical co-founder navigating the particular pressures of leading a mission-driven company — the tension between moving fast and getting it right, the emotional weight of the communities you serve — this is something we work on directly.

## How Mission-Driven Technical Strategy Differs: A Comparison

| Decision | General Startup Approach | Mission-Driven Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Data architecture | Optimize for product analytics and growth | Balance analytics with data minimization; consider what happens to user data if company fails |
| Vendor selection | Best-in-class for cost and features | Audit vendor data practices, pricing stability, and values alignment |
| Accessibility | Add after core product | Bake in from day one; accessibility is a mission requirement, not a feature |
| Security investment | Scale with company size | Earlier investment justified by user vulnerability and trust requirements |
| Technical debt | Manage against growth speed | Factor in mission continuity risk; debt that threatens uptime threatens communities you serve |
| Open source strategy | Cost and ecosystem benefits | Aligns with mission transparency; community contribution can be part of the mission |

**You're probably ready to talk if:**
- You have a working product and engineering team, and you've hit a technical inflection point — scaling, a new enterprise partner, a fundraise that requires technical diligence
- You're a non-technical or semi-technical founder who needs a senior technical voice in the room for key decisions

- You need someone who will tell you your technical strategy is fine when it isn't

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do you work with nonprofits or only for-profit companies?**

We work with both, but under different conditions. For nonprofits, we focus on organizations that have built internal technology products with engineering teams. If your nonprofit uses off-the-shelf tools and needs a technology consultant to help evaluate and implement them, we're not the right fit — there are firms that specialize in exactly that work and do it better than we would.

**What does "fractional CTO" mean in practice — how much of your time do we get?**

It varies by engagement, but typically 10–20 hours per week. More important than hours is availability: we're embedded, which means we're reachable when something comes up, not just at scheduled check-ins. We're clear about bandwidth in scoping calls and we don't over-commit.

**We're a B Corp. Do you have experience with the B Impact Assessment's technical implications?**

Yes. The B Impact Assessment has governance, data, and supply chain components that intersect with engineering decisions — vendor selection, data practices, and infrastructure transparency in particular. We've worked through these in practice.

**We have a technical co-founder. Do we still need a fractional CTO?**

Sometimes. The most common scenario where this makes sense: your technical co-founder is strong on execution but needs a senior thought partner for strategic decisions — architecture choices, key hires, investor conversations. We work well alongside technical co-founders in this mode. We also work well when a technical co-founder is carrying too much and needs someone to share the load for a defined period.

**Our investors are asking about our technical infrastructure. What can you do to help?**

Impact investors increasingly include technical diligence in their process, and enterprise partners in education, healthcare, and government ask detailed questions about security, compliance, and architecture. We've been in those rooms. We can help you prepare your technical narrative, review your infrastructure against likely diligence questions, and participate in conversations where a senior technical voice adds credibility.

**What's your pricing model?**

Monthly retainers, scoped to your needs. We don't publish rates because the right engagement varies significantly by stage, team size, and what you're trying to accomplish. We'd rather have a 30-minute conversation and give you an accurate number than quote something generic that doesn't fit.

**How long do engagements typically last?**

Most fractional CTO engagements run 6–18 months. Shorter if you're hiring a full-time CTO and need someone to bridge the gap or support the search. Longer if you're building steadily and don't need full-time leadership. We don't do retainers where we're just on call — every engagement has active work.

**Are you based in New York? Do you work with companies outside NYC?**

We're based in New York but work with companies across the US. Most of our work happens remotely, with periodic in-person for key moments — strategy sessions, team offsites, major hiring decisions. If you're in NYC and want someone who can be in the building regularly, we can accommodate that.
