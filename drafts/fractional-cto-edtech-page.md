## Why EdTech Engineering Is Different

Most fractional CTO services will tell you they work across industries. That's true — and for most technical challenges, it's fine. Architecture decisions, hiring frameworks, investor diligence prep: these are largely portable skills.

But EdTech has a set of requirements that generalists consistently underestimate:

**Student data privacy is not optional and not forgiving.** FERPA governs student education records at institutions receiving federal funding. COPPA governs data collection for children under 13. CIPA applies to schools receiving E-rate funding. Getting these wrong doesn't just create compliance risk — it ends contracts with school districts and destroys the trust your product depends on. A fractional CTO who hasn't lived through a school district procurement process may not know what questions the IT director is about to ask.

**Learning infrastructure has different scaling patterns than consumer apps.** EdTech products spike at back-to-school, at the start of semesters, and at exam periods — then go quiet. This isn't e-commerce traffic; it's predictable but extreme. Designing for this requires different decisions about caching, queuing, and infrastructure provisioning than a standard B2B SaaS product.

**LMS integrations are where EdTech products go to die.** Canvas, Schoology, Blackboard, Google Classroom, Moodle — every school district has its own setup, its own version, and its own IT policies. The LTI (Learning Tools Interoperability) standard helps, but the integration surface area is enormous. We've seen products stall for six months because the engineering team underestimated the Canvas integration. We've also seen it done right, in weeks.

**The product and engineering teams have to be tightly aligned on learning outcomes.** In most SaaS products, "the user wants to complete the task faster" is a reasonable proxy for value. In EdTech, the right outcome is often slower, more effortful, more friction — because learning requires struggle. An engineering team optimizing for engagement metrics in an EdTech product can actively undermine the educational value. This requires a technical leadership team that understands pedagogy, not just product.

## What a Fractional CTO Engagement Looks Like for an EdTech Startup

The specific mix depends on your stage and situation, but here's what we typically own in an EdTech fractional CTO engagement:

**Architecture and technical decision-making**
- Data architecture for student records (privacy-by-design, not privacy-by-retrofit)
- LMS integration strategy — which integrations to build first, how to build them so they scale, what to defer
- Build vs. buy decisions on assessment engines, video infrastructure, AI tutoring layers
- Infrastructure design for the EdTech usage spike pattern

**Engineering team leadership**
- Hiring: what roles to hire, how to evaluate candidates, what EdTech-specific experience actually matters vs. what doesn't
- Team structure: how to organize around the product, not just the technology
- Accountability: delivery cadence, technical debt triage, engineering culture

**Investor and partnership diligence**
- Preparing for the "how do you handle FERPA?" question — because investors at Series A will ask it and school district partners will make it a procurement requirement
- Technical roadmap that speaks to both product growth and compliance readiness

**Go-to-market technical support**
- School district sales support: technical security reviews, IT director Q&A, procurement questionnaires
- Partnership integrations: SSO, rostering (Clever, ClassLink), LMS connectors

## FAQ

**What EdTech-specific compliance should my startup be thinking about?**
FERPA applies to any product handling student education records at institutions receiving federal funding — which includes most K-12 schools and universities. COPPA applies to products collecting data from children under 13. CIPA applies to schools receiving E-rate funding. Most EdTech startups need at minimum a FERPA compliance review before entering school district sales. We help you understand what applies to your specific product and what decisions you need to make now vs. later.

**How is fractional CTO engagement different for EdTech vs. other sectors?**
The core engagement structure is the same — embedded leadership, 10–20 hours/week, retainer-based. What changes is the content: the compliance requirements, the integration landscape (LTI, Clever, ClassLink), the infrastructure patterns, and the product-engineering alignment around learning outcomes. We don't have to learn your industry — we've worked in it.

**Can a fractional CTO help with school district sales and procurement?**
Yes. School district IT procurement typically includes security questionnaires, data privacy agreements (DPAs), and sometimes IT director calls. We've been on those calls. We help you prepare your technical documentation, answer the hard questions, and build the compliance posture that enterprise EdTech customers require.

**Do you work with consumer EdTech or only institutional?**
Primarily institutional (K-12, higher ed, workforce development) where compliance, procurement, and learning infrastructure complexity are highest. Consumer EdTech without institutional customers is a better fit for a generalist fractional CTO unless there's a compelling reason to bring EdTech-specific expertise.

**What's the engagement timeline for an EdTech fractional CTO?**
Most engagements run 6–18 months. EdTech has a natural cadence tied to the academic calendar — we typically plan work in semester-aligned cycles, with heavier architecture and hiring work in the summer and fall, and heavier delivery focus during the academic year.

**How do you handle COPPA compliance for products with under-13 users?**
COPPA requires verifiable parental consent before collecting personal information from children under 13, and strict limits on data retention and sharing. The right implementation depends on your product — some companies build consent flows directly, others use a school-operator exception (where the school provides consent on behalf of parents). We help you understand which path is right for your product and what engineering decisions it requires.
