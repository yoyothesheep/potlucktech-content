## Quick Answer

You probably need a fractional CTO if you're spending money on engineers and can't evaluate whether it's working. Five specific triggers follow — if two or more apply, don't wait.

*Written by Sha-Mayn Teh, Co-Founder of Potluck Technologies and former engineering leader at Google (Google Translate) and TeachersPayTeachers, where she built and led a 50-person engineering organization.*

- The decision is symptom-based, not stage-based — you can be at Series A with none of these triggers, or seed with all five
- Two or more triggers applying simultaneously is the clearest signal to act
- Hiring a fractional CTO when you need a senior engineer is a mismatch — the section below explains the difference

## What are the 5 triggers that mean it's time to hire a fractional CTO? {#five-triggers}

These are operational pain signals, not stage milestones. The decision to hire a fractional CTO isn't about how much you've raised or how many engineers you have. It's about whether you have a specific set of problems that require senior technical leadership to solve.

You can be at seed stage with none of these triggers — in which case, wait. You can be at Series A with all five — in which case, you're already behind. Stage is a useful proxy, but it's a blunt one. The triggers below are more precise.

If two or more apply to you right now, the expected cost of waiting exceeds the cost of a fractional engagement. Keep reading.

---

## Trigger 1: You're spending money on engineers but can't evaluate whether it's working {#trigger-1}

This is the most common trigger — and the most expensive one to ignore.

Here's what it looks like in practice: your engineers are busy, standups are happening, tickets are moving. But you can't tell if the velocity is fast or slow for your stage. You can't tell if the architecture decisions being made today will cause problems in six months. You're not sure if your senior engineer is excellent or just confident. You nod along in technical discussions without really knowing if what's being described is the right call.

This is not a knowledge gap you can close by reading about software engineering. It's a judgment gap — the kind that takes years of building and shipping products to develop. A fractional CTO closes it.

Specifically, they establish delivery baselines (what should this team be shipping in a month?), put code review standards in place, create accountability structures, and give you a honest read on individual engineer performance. They're the person who can sit in a technical discussion and tell you afterward: "that was the right call" or "that's going to cost you in Q3."

**The compounding risk of waiting:** engineering habits calcify. A team that's been shipping slowly and without accountability for six months is harder to course-correct than one that's been doing it for six weeks. The fractional CTO engagement you push to Q2 will cost more in practice than the one you started in Q4.

## Trigger 2: Technical decisions are slowing your fundraising or go-to-market {#trigger-2}

You know this trigger is active when sentences like these start appearing in your week:

- "We can't launch that feature until we sort out the authentication architecture."
- "The investor asked how we'd scale to 10x users and I didn't have a good answer."
- "We need to decide between these two approaches but no one owns the decision."

These are not engineering problems. They are leadership problems. Engineering problems get solved by writing code. Leadership problems get solved by someone with enough authority and context to make the call, document the rationale, and move on.

The go-to-market version of this trigger is particularly costly because it has a direct revenue impact. Every week a feature is delayed because of an unresolved architectural debate is a week of pipeline your sales team doesn't have. A fractional CTO doesn't just make the decision — they create a decision-making structure so the next ten decisions don't back up the same way.

The fundraising version has a different shape. Investors doing technical diligence aren't just evaluating your architecture — they're evaluating whether you have someone who can answer hard questions with confidence. "We're working with a fractional CTO" is a more credible answer than "our lead engineer handles that." It signals that you know what you don't know.

## Trigger 3: You need technical co-founder credibility but haven't found — or can't afford — a full-time CTO {#trigger-3}

This trigger is common among non-technical founders who raised a pre-seed or seed round, hired a few engineers, and are now starting to feel the gap where a technical co-founder would be.

The gap shows up in a few ways: investors asking who owns the technical roadmap, enterprise prospects asking to speak with your CTO, recruiting conversations where senior engineering candidates want to know who they'd be reporting to.

A fractional CTO fills this gap functionally. They can be the person investors call with technical questions. They can run the technical portion of an enterprise sales call. They can be listed as technical leadership in your pitch deck — accurately, not misleadingly.

This is not a workaround. It's an appropriate use of the fractional model. The goal isn't to have a CTO title on an org chart — it's to have someone doing CTO-level work for your company at the hours and cost that match your current stage.

One distinction worth making: this trigger is different from "I have a senior engineer who is basically acting as CTO." That arrangement sounds similar but it creates real problems. A senior engineer promoted into a CTO-shaped role without the seniority, authority, or support structure for it tends to fail in ways that are hard to undo — both for the engineer and for the team. A fractional CTO sits above that arrangement, not as a replacement for the senior engineer but as genuine executive leadership.

## Trigger 4: You're approaching Series A and investors are asking hard technical questions {#trigger-4}

Series A diligence has a technical component that many founders underestimate until they're in it.

Investors will ask about: your architecture and how it scales, the state of your technical debt, your engineering team's capacity and hiring plan, your build vs. buy decisions on key infrastructure, and whether your technical roadmap is realistic given your team size. These questions don't have wrong answers — but they do have confident answers and uncertain ones.

A founder who has been working with a fractional CTO for six months before the Series A roadshow walks into those conversations with documented answers. Architecture decisions have a rationale. Technical debt has been triaged and quantified. The engineering hiring plan is tied to the product roadmap. This preparation is not cosmetic — investors are trying to assess execution risk, and organized technical leadership reduces it.

**The timing issue:** this trigger has a lead time. If you start a fractional CTO engagement the week before your Series A pitch, you won't get the benefit — the fractional CTO won't have enough context to prepare you well. The right timing is 3–6 months before you expect to be in diligence. That's enough time for the engagement to produce real artifacts investors can evaluate.

## Trigger 5: Technical debt is threatening your roadmap {#trigger-5}

Technical debt is normal. All software accumulates it, especially software built quickly by a small team trying to find product-market fit. The problem isn't having technical debt — it's when technical debt starts blocking forward progress.

For a non-technical founder, the symptom looks like this: your engineers keep saying "before we can build X, we need to refactor Y." The list of things that need to be refactored before you can ship new features keeps growing. Your quarterly roadmap has started including items like "infrastructure cleanup" or "platform migration" that your engineers say are urgent but that have no direct customer value.

This is not an engineering problem that more engineers will solve. Adding headcount to an engineering organization with unresolved technical debt typically makes it worse — more engineers writing new code on top of a shaky foundation, faster.

What's needed is a prioritization decision: which technical debt is actually blocking the roadmap, which can be addressed incrementally, and which can be left alone safely. That's a leadership decision, not an engineering one. A fractional CTO triage the debt, sequences the paydown against new feature work, and gives you a realistic picture of what you can ship and when.

## When should you NOT hire a fractional CTO? {#when-not-to}

Not every technical problem requires a fractional CTO. Hiring one when you need something else is an expensive mismatch.

**Your problem is headcount, not leadership.** If your engineers are working well and you just need more of them to hit your roadmap, you need to hire engineers — not add executive overhead. A fractional CTO won't write code for your product.

**You have a strong technical co-founder who needs a sounding board.** If your technical co-founder is competent but occasionally wants a peer to pressure-test decisions with, an advisor or a fractional advisory arrangement is probably more appropriate than a fractional CTO engagement. The latter involves ongoing leadership and accountability structures that aren't necessary if the problem is just "no one to sanity-check my architecture decisions."

**You're pre-product with no engineers yet.** If you haven't hired your first engineer, you don't need a fractional CTO — you need help with your first engineering hire and some early architecture thinking. That's often better handled through an advisory engagement or a single project-scoped consultation, not an ongoing fractional leadership arrangement.

**You want someone to write code.** Fractional CTOs lead engineering organizations. They don't build your product. If you need an experienced engineer to build alongside your team, that's a senior contract engineer or a development shop — not a fractional CTO.

**You've already crossed 20+ engineers.** At this scale, the coordination overhead of a fractional arrangement starts to exceed its value. You likely need a full-time CTO. [The stage-by-stage comparison in our guide to fractional vs. full-time CTOs](/blog/fractional-cto-vs-full-time-cto) walks through exactly when that line is crossed.

## What's the difference between needing a fractional CTO and needing a senior engineer? {#vs-senior-engineer}

This is one of the most useful questions to answer before you hire, because getting it wrong is expensive in both directions.

| Problem | What you need |
|---|---|
| Not enough code getting written | Senior engineer or contractor |
| Code getting written but no one owning architecture | Fractional CTO |
| Engineers lack direction on what to build | Fractional CTO (with product input) |
| Engineers are productive but you can't evaluate them | Fractional CTO |
| No one to run technical interviews | Fractional CTO (or senior engineer + structured process) |
| Technical debt slowing delivery | Fractional CTO to triage, engineers to execute |
| Investor technical diligence | Fractional CTO |
| You need more engineering capacity | Senior engineer or contractors |

The simplest version of the rule: if the problem is about *how much* work is getting done, you need engineering capacity. If the problem is about *whether the right work is getting done, and whether it's being done well*, you need leadership.

**The first-engineer-as-CTO trap**

A pattern worth naming directly: many early-stage startups promote their first or most senior engineer into a de facto CTO role — giving them the title, or treating them as one, without the authority structure, seniority, or support that role requires.

This tends to fail in predictable ways. The engineer is now expected to make executive decisions, manage their peers, interface with investors, and write code — simultaneously, with no additional support. The role is set up to overwhelm them. When it does, you lose a good engineer, damage a team relationship, and still don't have CTO-level leadership.

A fractional CTO sitting above this arrangement changes the dynamic. The senior engineer keeps the scope they're actually equipped for. The fractional CTO handles executive-level decisions, investor interface, and team leadership. Both succeed.

## How do you run a 30-day test to validate a fractional CTO engagement? {#thirty-day-test}

Before you commit to a 6-month engagement, a 30-day scoped test is a reasonable way to validate fit — both whether the fractional CTO is right for your company and whether a fractional CTO is actually what you need.

**Week 1: Define the three problems you actually need solved.**

Be specific. "We need technical leadership" is not a problem — it's a category. "Our engineers have been debating the database architecture for six weeks with no resolution, and it's blocking the Q2 roadmap" is a problem. Write down three of these. If you can't name three specific problems, that's useful data: you may not be ready for a fractional CTO engagement yet.

**Weeks 2–3: What does meaningful progress look like?**

For each of your three problems, define what "better" looks like at day 30. Not solved — better. The database architecture debate has a decision and a documented rationale. Investor technical prep has a first draft of the architecture overview. Engineering velocity has a baseline measurement and a weekly reporting cadence. These are reasonable 30-day deliverables for a competent fractional CTO engagement.

**Day 30: Three evaluation questions.**

1. Is engineering team velocity improving — more shipped, fewer blockers, better estimates?
2. Are investor or customer technical questions getting cleaner, more confident answers?
3. Do architectural decisions have a documented owner and a clear rationale?

If two of three are yes, the engagement is working. If none are yes, something is wrong — either the fractional CTO isn't the right fit, or the problem you thought you had isn't the actual problem.

One honest note: if day 30 reveals that what you actually needed was more engineering headcount, not leadership, that's a good outcome too. Better to know at day 30 than at month 6.

## FAQ {#faq}

**Is it too early to hire a fractional CTO if I have no engineers yet?**

Probably yes. Without an engineering team, there's no organization to lead. What you likely need at this stage is help thinking through your first engineering hire — what role to hire, how to evaluate candidates, what to pay. That's a scoped advisory engagement, not an ongoing fractional leadership arrangement. Most fractional CTOs will tell you the same thing if you describe your situation honestly.

**What if I can't afford a fractional CTO?**

The going rate for a fractional CTO is $9,000–$18,000 per month for a 10–20 hour weekly commitment. If that's genuinely out of reach at your current stage, a few alternatives worth considering: a shorter-scoped diagnostic engagement (some fractional CTOs offer a fixed-fee architecture audit or hiring review), an advisory arrangement at lower hours and cost, or waiting until you have the capital to do it properly. A cut-rate fractional CTO engagement — too few hours, wrong experience level — tends to create false confidence. If you can't fund it properly, it may be better to wait.

**How quickly can a fractional CTO start delivering value?**

2–4 weeks for early signal; 60–90 days for meaningful organizational change. The fast wins — architecture decision that's been stuck, investor technical prep, first engineering hiring process — can happen in the first few weeks. Changing team habits, velocity, and culture takes longer. A full-time CTO hire typically takes 3–6 months to reach the same early-signal point.

**Can I hire a fractional CTO partway through a fundraise?**

Yes, and it's often the right move if you're already in conversations and realizing you're underprepared on the technical side. The fractional CTO won't have deep context on your codebase yet, but they can rapidly prepare you for specific investor questions, review your architecture overview, and be present for technical diligence calls. It's not ideal timing — 3–6 months before the fundraise is better — but mid-raise is better than not at all.

**How do I vet a fractional CTO before hiring?**

Look for someone who has worked with multiple companies at your specific stage, not just large organizations. Ask for examples of architectural decisions they've made and what happened afterward. Ask what engagements they've ended and why. The willingness to name failures and limitations is a stronger signal than a clean track record. Red flags: fractional CTOs who want to stay fractional forever (the goal should be to build toward a full-time hire when the time is right), those who can't name specific deliverables from past engagements, and those who are running more than 6–8 concurrent engagements.
