# LinkedIn Content

**Voice:** Read `BRAND_VOICE_NOTES` from CONFIG.md before writing. Apply those rules throughout.

---

## What This Skill Is For

Writing LinkedIn posts that announce site launches, new content sections, or platform milestones. The goal is **genuine reach** — the kind that comes from writing something worth sharing, not engineering engagement through hooks and tricks.

LinkedIn's algorithm rewards posts that hold attention and generate meaningful replies. Not shallow reactions. Our version of going viral is: someone in the comments sharing their own story. A DM from a recruiter. A career changer sharing the post because it named something they'd felt but hadn't articulated.

---

## Format Constraints

LinkedIn has specific mechanics worth knowing:

- **The fold is after ~3 lines.** The opening must earn the expand tap. Do this by naming something specific and true — not a teaser, not a cliffhanger.
- **No links in the first comment** (this is a myth). In the post body, a link is fine, but put it near the end after the post has earned it.
- **Line breaks are your friend.** Short paragraphs. White space. One idea per block.
- **Images stop the scroll.** Use a graphic. Every post should have one.
- **No hashtag wall.** 2–3 max, at the very end, and only if they're specific. `#careerdevelopment` is fine. `#AI #careers #jobs #work #future` is not.

## The Opening

The opening does one job: **make the reader feel seen**. Not impressed. Not hooked. Seen.

The most durable LinkedIn openings are either:
1. A specific, concrete fact that reframes something the reader thought they understood
2. A one-sentence human truth that lands before the explanation

**Opening examples:**

> Entry-level software jobs are down 78% from their April 2022 peak.
> Senior postings fell too, but they've mostly recovered.
> Junior roles haven't.

> We've had over 500,000 people visit this site in the last few months.
> Almost all of them look at more than one career.
> People aren't casually browsing — they're working something out.

> A software engineer in Japan finished a piece of AI-generated code, checked the output, got it right, and left the office on time to pick up his daughter from daycare.
> He said it was the first time AI had matched him on a real business task.
> That story is from Anthropic's 81k-person study of how people are actually using AI.

Notice what these don't do: they don't frame their own significance. They state the thing and let you decide what to make of it.

**Opening anti-patterns — these read as AI-written:**

- "That's not a projection. That's happening right now." — the rhetorical repetition to punch up a fact
- "That told us something." — vague teaser that promises meaning without delivering it
- "That's the kind of moment this shift produces." — editorializing a quote into a thesis
- "— not reassurance, but data." — the em-dash pivot/reversal construction that sounds crisp but means very little
- Any sentence that end-caps a fact with a one-liner designed to make it feel more significant than the fact already is

If a fact needs a follow-up sentence to tell the reader it's important, the fact isn't specific enough yet. Get more specific instead.

**What to avoid in the opening:**
- Questions: "Have you ever wondered...?" — signals nothing, often feels manipulative
- Personal address: "If you're a [job title], this matters to you" — too narrow, too easy to dismiss
- The announcement-first construction: "Excited to share that we just launched..." — nobody cares yet

## Using Real Human Quotes

The Anthropic 81k interviews (https://www.anthropic.com/features/81k-interviews) are a legitimate citation source for how real people are experiencing the AI shift. These quotes are rich, specific, and unpolished in the best way. Use them to ground the post in lived experience, not abstract concern.

### How to use them right

Pick one quote that **reflects what readers are already quietly feeling** — then build the post *from* that feeling, not toward it. Quote first, then connect to site context.

**Good use:**
> "In the third industrial revolution, horses disappeared from city streets, replaced by automobiles. Now people are afraid that they're the horses."
> — Someone currently out of work, United States (Anthropic 81k interviews)
>
> That fear makes sense. We built this site to give people specific, sourced answers about which roles are actually at risk — and which projections are based on real labor data.
> [link]

**Bad use (over-explaining):**
> This quote really shows us how SCARED people are about AI and why our tool is MORE IMPORTANT THAN EVER...

### Quote selection principles

Pick quotes that:
- Name a specific, concrete feeling or situation (not abstract concern)
- Come from someone in or adjacent to a career the post is about
- Don't require setup to land — they should work on their own

Avoid quotes that:
- Sound exaggerated or melodramatic out of context
- Are about topics too far from the post's focus
- Need heavy editorial framing to seem relevant

### Relevant quotes by audience

**For career-changers and people worried about their job:**
> "In the third industrial revolution, horses disappeared from city streets, replaced by automobiles. Now people are afraid that they're the horses."
> — Not currently working, United States

> "Yes, at my old job, they replaced me as a writer with an AI."
> — [unnamed]

**For entry-level / recent grads:**
> "I got excellent grades using AI's answers, not what I'd actually learned. I just memorized what AI gave me... That's when I feel the most self-reproach."
> — South Korea

**For people finding a path forward:**
> "I've been living in a homeless shelter... AI helped me brainstorm ways to brand myself for my digital marketing business. I want to turn my finances around, and get a house. AI is helping me see a path I hadn't considered before."
> — Healthcare worker, United States

> "For the first time, I felt AI had surpassed human quality in a business task. That day I left work on time and picked up my daughter from daycare."
> — Software engineer, Japan

**For people feeling economically squeezed:**
> "I'm in a tech-disadvantaged country, and I can't afford many failures. With AI, I've reached professional level in cybersecurity, UX design, marketing, and project management simultaneously. Finding a payment platform available in my region would have taken me a month. AI did it in 30 seconds. It's an equalizer."
> — Entrepreneur, Cameroon

## Graphics

Every post should have an image. Text-only posts perform worse in feed and look unfinished.

**What works on LinkedIn:**

| Type | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Data screenshot** | When announcing research or labor market numbers. Pull a chart directly from the career guides or industry pages. |
| **Career score table** | For industry deep-dive announcements. The layered score table is immediately scannable and interesting to people in that field. |
| **Side-by-side stat** | Two numbers that tell a story: "Junior dev postings: –78%. Senior postings: –50%, recovering." Clean, dark background, minimal text. |
| **A real quote card** | A human quote from the Anthropic 81k interviews, typeset cleanly. Light background, name/context below. Not overlaid on a stock photo. |
| **The site itself** | A screenshot of a career page or industry landing — shows what you're launching, not just talking about it. |

**Graphic tone:**
- Same visual language as the site (clean, minimal, not corporate)
- No clip art, no gradient "brand kit" backgrounds
- No inspiration-quote aesthetics (script fonts, sunsets)
- If using a quote card: plain background, readable font, attribution included

## Post Structure

### Short Form (for smaller announcements or sharing a specific piece of data)

```
[Opening — 1–2 lines. Specific and true.]

[The thing you're announcing or sharing — 2–3 lines. What it is, in plain language.]

[One human truth or quote — 2–3 lines. A real story or data point that grounds it.]

[The honest close — 1–2 lines. What to do with this, or what it means.]

[Link]
[2–3 hashtags]
```

### Long Form (for major launches)

```
[Opening — 1–2 lines. Earns the expand.]

[Set the scene — 2–4 lines. What's been happening in the labor market / what drove this.]

[The announcement — 2–4 lines. What launched, specifically. No jargon.]

[One quote or human moment — 3–5 lines. With attribution.]

[What it shows / why it matters — 2–3 lines. The honest interpretation. No spin.]

[Invitation — 1–2 lines. A low-pressure call to read, comment, or share if it resonates. Not a hard CTA.]

[Link]
[2–3 hashtags]
```

## What Makes It Shareable (Our Way)

LinkedIn posts get shared when they do one of three things:

1. **Name something the reader has felt but hasn't heard named clearly.** The "horses" quote works because it's exact. The data on junior dev postings works because it's specific and verifiable.

2. **Give someone something useful to pass along.** A career guide. A data table. A clear answer to a question someone they know is asking.

3. **Make someone feel seen in a situation that often goes unacknowledged.** The person worried about their entry-level role. The mid-career worker wondering if their skills still matter. Writing *for* them (not about them) makes them want to share.

**We do not do:**
- "Drop a 🙋 in the comments if you..." polling mechanics
- "Share this if you agree" explicit asks
- Manufactured controversy or hot takes to drive replies
- Engagement bait leading paragraphs

## Pre-Post Checklist

- [ ] Does the opening give something specific, not just promise something interesting?
- [ ] Does any sentence follow a fact just to announce that the fact matters? Cut it.
- [ ] Check for these AI-writing patterns: "That's not a projection", "That told us something", "That's the kind of X this Y produces", em-dash pivots to a moral. Rewrite.
- [ ] Is there a graphic? Does it add something, or is it decorative?
- [ ] If using a human quote: is it attributed with enough context? Does it land without heavy framing?
- [ ] Is the link near the end, not the beginning?
- [ ] Does the close feel like a natural end, not a CTA?
- [ ] Fewer than 4 hashtags?
- [ ] No "crushed it" / "don't get left behind" / "it's not X it's Y" constructions? (See `now-page-voice/SKILL.md`)
- [ ] Read it aloud. Would you share this if a peer had written it?
